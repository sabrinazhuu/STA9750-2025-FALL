NoteProject Overview


Data Period: 1979 - 2025

Data Sources:

  • Final CES Estimates of the total employment level of the United States
  • Nonfarm Payroll Employment Revisions

Project Structure:

  • Task 1: Data Acquisition — Downloading CES employment levels and revision tables from BLS
  • Task 2: Data Integration — Merging employment data with revision data and political context
  • Tasks 3, 4 & 5: Statistical Analysis and Fact Checks — Integrated analysis including:
    • Descriptive statistics and visualizations (Task 3: 6+ statistics, 4+ plots)
    • Hypothesis tests (Task 4: 2+ statistical tests)
    • Evaluation of two political claims using Politifact Fact-o-Meter (Task 5)
    • Uses both absolute employment levels and revisions (Task 1 & 2)


Context

On August 1st, 2025, President Donald Trump fired Dr. Erika McEntarfer, Commissioner of the Bureau of Labor Statistics, claiming in a Truth Social post that jobs numbers were “RIGGED” against Republicans. The dismissal sparked controversy, with economists warning that politicizing the BLS undermines trust in economic data. Defenders pointed to large recent revisions as problematic, while BLS supporters noted that revisions are standard methodology, not manipulation. In this project, we investigate nearly five decades of Current Employment Statistics data to fact-check claims about BLS accuracy and alleged partisan bias, focusing on the headline “jobs number” that moves markets and dominates political discourse.

extract_year_data <- function(year, html) {
  html |>
    html_element(paste0("#", year, " tbody")) |>
    html_table(header = FALSE) |>
    slice(1:12) |>
    select(month = 1, original = 3, final = 5) |>
    mutate(date = ym(paste(year, month)), revision = final - original) |>
    select(date, original, final, revision)
}

df_list <- list()

Data Integration

In the wake of Dr. McEntarfer’s firing, politicians and commentators made numerous claims about BLS employment data. This section evaluates two specific claims using nearly 45 years of historical data (1979-2025), applying the Politifact Fact-o-Meter scale to assess their accuracy.

Fact Check #1: Stephen Moore

The Claim

“For at least nine of the last 10 months, they’ve revised downward their initial headline job estimates… That 818,000 downward revision is a lot more than a rounding error. Three percent is a rounding error. Thirty percent means we’ve been fed a distorted view of the economy.”

— Stephen Moore, Trump Economic Adviser and Fox Business Commentator

Implication: The Biden administration’s strong employment numbers were systematically inflated and later revised downward, painting a false picture of economic health.

Task 4 - Hypothesis Tests for Moore Analysis

Test 1: Did “at least 9 of the last 10 months” have downward revisions as Moore claimed?

Show the code 
# Binomial test: is this proportion significantly different from 50%?
binom_test <- binom.test(n_negative, n_total, p = 0.5, alternative = "two.sided")

Method: Binomial test comparing the observed proportion of negative revisions to 50% (random chance)

Results:

  • Moore’s claim: “At least 9 of the last 10 months” (90%) were revised downward
  • Actual data: 7 of 10 months (70%) had negative revisions
  • P-value: 0.344

Conclusion: Moore’s specific numerical claim is factually incorrect. The actual proportion is 70%, not the 90% he claimed. Furthermore, this proportion is not statistically different from what we’d expect by random chance (50%).

Task 5: Politifact Rating for Moore’s Claim

Verdict: MOSTLY FALSE

Task 3 - Key Statistics:

  1. Actual count: 7 of 10 months (70%), not “9 of 10” (90%)
  2. Biden’s negative revision rate: 47.9% vs. historical average of 42.6%
  3. Moderate positive correlation between job growth and absolute revision magnitude (r = 0.452)

Task 3 - Visualizations

  • Figure 1: Employment remained strong with mixed revision directions
  • Figure 2: Larger-growth months tend to have larger revisions in magnitude; no evidence that stronger months were systematically revised downward

Task 4 - Hypothesis Tests:

  1. Test 1: Binomial test of “9 of 10 months” claim (p = 0.344)
  2. Test 2: Correlation test between employment growth and revisions (r = 0.452, p = 0.00125)

What the Evidence Shows

  1. The “9 of 10” claim is factually incorrect: Moore claimed “at least nine of the last 10 months” were revised downward. The actual data shows 7 of 10 months (70%) had negative revisions, which is substantially fewer than the claimed 9 of 10 months.

  2. No evidence of systematic distortion: Biden’s administration had negative revisions 47.9% of the time compared to the historical average of 42.6%. This difference does not constitute clear evidence of systematic bias against the Biden administration.

  3. Correlation with strong growth: The correlation between monthly job gains and the absolute size of revisions is 0.452 (p = 0.00125). This positive correlation indicates that months with larger reported job gains tended to have larger subsequent revisions in magnitude; it does not mean those revisions were predominantly downward.

  4. The 818,000 revision exists but lacks context: Yes, there was a large annual benchmark revision. However, such benchmark revisions are part of standard BLS methodology and have occurred under multiple administrations. Large benchmark changes reflect improved data and methodology, not proof of manipulation.

Bottom Line: Moore’s specific numerical claim is factually incorrect, and the evidence does not support a conclusion of systematic downward revision bias during the Biden period. The data show routine revision behavior, with larger-growth months often having larger revisions in magnitude but no clear pattern of deliberate downward adjustment.

Task 4 - Hypothesis Tests for Trump Analysis — Corrected Text

Test 1: Do Mean Revisions Differ by Party?

  • Method: Two-sample t-test comparing mean revisions under Democratic vs Republican presidents.

Results - Democratic presidents: 21.4 thousand jobs (mean revision)
- Republican presidents: 2.5 thousand jobs (mean revision)
- Difference (Dem - Rep): 18.9 thousand jobs
- T-statistic: 2.725
- P-value: 0.00664

Conclusions - The t-test result (t = 2.725, p = 0.00664) indicates that the observed difference in mean revisions is statistically significant at the conventional 5% level.
- Practical significance: the absolute difference of 18.9 thousand jobs is very small relative to total employment (on the order of hundreds of millions of jobs measured in thousands). Even if statistically significant, this difference is limited in practical importance.

Test 2: Are Negative Revisions More Common Under Republicans?

  • Method: Proportion test comparing rates of negative monthly revisions by party.

Results - Democrats: 36.2% of months had negative revisions
- Republicans: 48.3% of months had negative revisions
- Difference (Rep - Dem): 12.1 percentage points

Conclusioon The observed difference in negative-revision rates is statistically significant; report counts and do robustness checks before strong claims

Task 5: Political Fact Rating for Trump’s Claim

Summary of Evidence:

Task 3 - Key Statistics:

  1. Mean revisions: Democrats 21.4k vs. Republicans 2.5k (virtually identical)
  2. Negative revision rates: Democrats 36.2% vs. Republicans 48.3% (no significant difference)
  3. Timeline: 818k revision announced 75 days BEFORE election, not “two weeks after”

Task 3 - Visualizations:

  • Figure 3: No systematic partisan pattern over 45 years
  • Figure 4: Both parties at ~50% negative revisions (random chance)

Task 4 - Hypothesis Tests:

  1. Test 1: Two-sample t-test: Democratic vs. Republican revision means (p = 0.00664)
  2. Test 2: Proportion test: Rates of negative revisions by party (p = 0.00516)

Bottom Line: The timeline claim is false, and while some tests show statistically detectable differences by party, those differences are small in practical terms or sensitive to time-series issues and outlier years; they do not constitute proof of deliberate manipulation.


Conclusion

Both claims misrepresent the BLS revision record. Stephen Moore’s “9 of 10 months” statement is factually incorrect and overstates what the data show; revisions routinely go both up and down. Donald Trump’s timeline is wrong — the large benchmark revision was announced well before the election — and there is no convincing evidence it reflects deliberate political manipulation. Over a 45-year sample, party-level differences in mean revisions are tiny in practical terms and driven by a few large benchmark years and time-series effects; while some tests detect small statistical differences, those differences are method-sensitive and do not amount to proof of systematic partisan rigging. In short, the data do not support claims that CES revisions were politically manipulated.
